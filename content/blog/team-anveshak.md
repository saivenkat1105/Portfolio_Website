If I had to describe my 3 years at IIT Madras with a single word, it would be **Anveshak**. Joining the team was one of the best decisions I ever made, both for me and my resume. My journey with the team has been really incredible with loads of memories, inside jokes, foosball, robotics and whatnot.

## What is Team Anveshak?

We are a group of 20+ undergraduate students with a passion for robotics. We are on a mission to build the next generation of Mars Rovers that could one day aid astronauts in space. We participate in the international **University Rover Challenge (URC)** conducted by the Mars Society — a competition aimed at encouraging university students to pursue robotics for space exploration.

We work out of the **Centre for Innovation (CFI)**, a dedicated maker space at IIT Madras open to student teams like ours.

## My Journey

### Team Member (Nov 2017 – Jun 2018)

After a 3-month process of tests and mini projects, I got into the team. As a fresher, my work was basically buying stuff from outside, machining and assembling parts, tightening bolts, and — most importantly — filing the parts that didn't fit. It was painful, but it gave me a real understanding of design-for-manufacturability: the kind of problems you only discover when your hands are covered in metal shavings.

### Chassis Design Team — *Caesar* (Jun 2018 – Jun 2019)

This was the year I was actually working on the design of our rover, which we named **Caesar**. My main contribution was designing the **rocker-bogie suspension system** — a mechanism that allows the rover to traverse rough terrain by distributing weight dynamically across all six wheels.

![Caesar rover at IRC 2019](/blog/caesar.jpg)

I was also part of the team that developed a completely autonomous **soil collection and testing laboratory** to be used onboard. And I handled most of the manufacturing to get the rover completed in time for competition.

**Results:**
- 🥇 Won the **Indian Rover Challenge (IRC) 2019** — in our very first attempt
- 🌍 Placed **12th internationally** at the **University Rover Challenge 2019** out of 90+ teams

### Team Lead — *Dark Knight* (Jul 2019 – May 2020)

After returning from URC 2019, we started work on our next rover almost immediately. I became the **Chassis Design Lead** and **Finance Lead**, managing a budget of INR 7.85 lakhs. The rover was named **Dark Knight** (I wanted it to be *Dora the Explorer*, but my teammates weren't creative enough).

![Dark Knight rover](/blog/dark_knight.jpg)

Managing the team, handling differences of opinion, and figuring out the path forward were things I genuinely grew into during this phase. Ideation, discussions, and endless deliberations went into every design decision.

Then COVID-19 happened and cancelled everything.

**Results:**
- Placed **5th** at IRC 2020 — a below-par performance due to some competition mistakes
- Placed **8th** after the qualification phase of URC 2020 (report + video submission)

## The Competitions

### IRC 2019 — *The Championship*

After a rough performance at URC 2018, we decided to compete at IRC 2019 to sharpen ourselves for URC 2019. The competition was held at Manipal Institute of Technology.

We barely finished the rover in time and entered with **zero expectations**. None of us thought we'd win.

But we did.

> "He gave a long pause, asked the audience who they thought the winner was, gave another long pause just to mess with us. Then he announced our names and everyone literally jumped from their seats."

That moment — everyone erupting — is a memory I'll carry forever.

### URC 2019 — *Utah, USA*

After IRC, I think we got a little overconfident. We were complacent, and it showed. The competition was held in **Hanksville, Utah** — my first time leaving India.

We performed decently for two days, then faltered on Day 3's science task. We placed **12th**, losing 11th by just 0.3 points. Heartbreaking.

But we toured Salt Lake City the next day, trekked to the top of Ensign Peak, and then explored LA and Las Vegas. We soon forgot the disappointment.

### IRC 2020 — *The Comeback That Wasn't*

We entered the 2020 season with a lot of preparation and finished manufacturing early. Competition was at VIT, Chennai.

Day 1 went brilliantly. Then we blew up our motherboard on Day 2. We recovered on Day 3 and ended up **5th** — not the result we wanted.

Then Corona took over the world.

## Life at CFI

My daily routine: wake up, go to classes, end up at CFI by evening, leave at midnight, grab Maggi and an egg sandwich from Ramu's.

There were days I never went to my room for 3–4 days straight — sleeping in CFI as deadlines hit. We worked, played foosball, debated everything from Ram Mandir to Game of Thrones. We watched IPL finals and GOT episodes right there. When the institute had a water crisis and accommodation was unavailable, CFI became my home for two weeks.

The pain of manufacturing is beyond words — procurement trips to Ekkatuthangal, Ambatthur, Parry's across Chennai in buses powered by the Tamil language (of which I speak exactly zero words). Special mention to **JNS Metals** and **Sree Balamurugan Engineering Enterprises** for making those joyrides unforgettable.

---

## What I Took Away

I cannot imagine what my insti life would have looked like without Team Anveshak. The original motivation was, honestly, the free US trip (which turned out not to be free). But I have absolutely no regrets.

The technical knowledge — IK models, suspension dynamics, CAD, manufacturing — is unmatched by any coursework. But more than that, I learned **how to build things with people**, how to fail and keep going, and how to find meaning in the chaos of deadlines and broken parts and impossible timelines.

3 years at Anveshak was a roller coaster ride. And if I could, I'd take it again and again and again.
